Root Cause Analysis Guide:
Understanding Why Initiatives Stall

A structured guide for program and department leads to explore why teams are slow, reluctant, or resistant to adopting best practices and improvements.
How to use this guide — digital health & data systems

When a data or digital health initiative stalls, the reason most often given is that the team or department simply doesn't have time for it. It's worth pausing on that explanation — because "we don't have time" is rarely the full picture. What it usually means is that the team hasn't been able to prioritize this initiative over the other demands on their time. That's a meaningfully different problem, and one we can do something about.

This version is oriented toward initiatives involving data collection, data quality, digital tools, EMR use, reporting, and information systems. Use it when a team or department is struggling to adopt or sustain a data-related practice — whether that's completing records accurately, using a reporting platform consistently, or participating in a data review process.

Set aside the "no time" explanation and work through the root causes below. For each one that resonates, dig into the sub-questions to identify what's actually in the way.

Starting question — reframe "we don't have time" as:
Why is the team or department not able to prioritize X over their other work?

Dig deeper: why does no accountability exist?
Why this happens
Data quality is often treated as a technical concern owned by an M&E or informatics team — frontline teams aren't measured on it and may not know they're expected to be
Reporting dashboards may track coverage or outcomes but not the completeness or accuracy of the underlying records
Recommended actions
Add a simple data completeness indicator to existing review meetings — even tracking one critical field (e.g. diagnosis recorded, follow-up date entered) creates visibility
Share a completeness report with team leads regularly so the issue is visible before it becomes a problem for reporting
Frame accountability positively at first — recognition for improvement rather than consequences for gaps
Why this happens
Teams receive regular requests for data submissions but rarely hear feedback on quality — the implicit message is that submitting anything is sufficient
Data quality initiatives from an M&E or informatics team may not carry the same weight as directives from clinical or program leadership
Recommended actions
Request that program or clinical leadership explicitly endorse data quality as a priority — not just data submission — in team communications and reviews
Close the feedback loop: share what happens to data after it is submitted, so teams understand that quality affects decisions, not just compliance
Why this happens
Teams are measured on whether reports are submitted on time, not on whether the data in them is complete or correct — so speed takes precedence over quality
Corrections and re-submissions are rarely required, reinforcing the idea that incomplete data is acceptable
Recommended actions
Introduce a quality check step before submission is considered complete — even a brief peer review of key fields changes the incentive
Where feasible, flag submissions with critical missing fields and return them for completion before accepting them as final
Distinguish between submission compliance and data quality in reporting — make both visible separately
Dig deeper: why is value not apparent?
Why this happens
Frontline staff enter data into systems that generate reports they never see — the value flows to program managers, donors, or policymakers, not to the people doing the work
When data entry feels like paperwork rather than a clinical or operational tool, it competes poorly with direct patient care
Recommended actions
Create a local feedback product — even a simple monthly summary showing the team's own trends — so data entry produces something visible and useful at their level
Ask the team: "What question about your patients would you most like to be able to answer?" Then show how better data quality makes that possible
Where feasible, use the data to surface something actionable for the team — a patient due for follow-up, a gap in a protocol — so data entry has an immediate local payoff
Why this happens
Data may be collected and reported but teams rarely hear how it was used — decisions made using their data are invisible to them
If data is only ever cited in problems ("your numbers are low") rather than solutions ("because of your data, we were able to..."), trust in its value erodes
Recommended actions
Make a point of closing the loop: when program data influences a resource decision, policy change, or guideline update, tell the teams whose data contributed
Share a concrete example: "The data you submitted last quarter showed us X, which led to Y" — even one example per year changes the perception of data's purpose
Why this happens
Health programs often introduce new data systems with each grant cycle, requiring teams to learn new platforms repeatedly without continuity
Systems are sometimes deployed, used briefly, and then abandoned — leaving teams unwilling to invest in the next one
Recommended actions
Acknowledge the history directly: "I know we've asked you to learn new systems before — here's what's different and why this one is worth the investment"
Provide a credible commitment to continuity — a timeline, a sustainability plan, or leadership endorsement signaling this is not a short-term pilot
Deliver an early, visible win from the system — something the team finds useful within the first 60 days
Dig deeper: what is blocking implementation?
Why this happens
Data quality improvement is often described in abstract terms — "improve completeness," "ensure accuracy" — without a concrete workflow or process to follow
There may be no designated data focal point on the team, so responsibility for driving change is diffuse
Recommended actions
Offer a concrete starting workflow: e.g. a weekly 20-minute record review by a designated person, using a simple completeness checklist
Name a data champion or focal point within the team — even informally — to own the process
Check in at 30 days with the focal point to troubleshoot early friction before it becomes a reason to stop
Why this happens
Data quality is often owned by an M&E officer or records clerk who has no authority over clinical staff documentation practices
Clinicians may not see data entry as part of their clinical role — and without a senior clinical voice endorsing it, they won't change their behavior
Recommended actions
Engage the medical director, head nurse, or clinical lead to frame complete documentation as a clinical responsibility, not just an administrative one
Reframe the ask: "complete records support continuity of care for your patients" lands differently than "please fill in all fields"
Explore whether documentation standards can be included in clinical supervision or peer review processes already in place
Why this happens
EMR and reporting platforms often assume a level of digital comfort that not all staff have — errors and incomplete records may be symptoms of skill gaps, not carelessness
Initial training may have covered system basics but not the specific fields or workflows most prone to error
Recommended actions
Identify the fields with the highest error or omission rates and provide targeted, role-specific guidance on those — not a full system retraining
Create a one-page visual job aid for the most common documentation tasks, kept at the point of entry
Build basic system use into staff onboarding so new hires start with the skill rather than learning on the job
Why this happens
Understaffed teams managing high patient volumes have no genuine bandwidth for process change, regardless of motivation
Data or digital initiatives may arrive on top of clinical or administrative demands that are already at capacity
Recommended actions
Have an honest conversation about current load before adding X — and be willing to sequence rather than stack initiatives
Identify the single highest-impact data quality change and focus there first; resist the urge to fix everything at once
If capacity is genuinely the constraint, bring this to leadership as a resourcing issue — not a motivation or skill gap
Note: proceeding without addressing capacity typically produces a brief burst of compliance followed by reversion. It is worth naming the constraint directly.
Dig deeper: what is the infrastructure gap?
Why this happens
Digital tools are often designed assuming consistent electricity and internet — conditions that many care delivery locations don't have
Devices may be shared across shifts or departments, making consistent data entry at the point of care impractical
Recommended actions
Audit actual infrastructure before deployment: power reliability, connectivity, device availability, and system uptime — document the real baseline
Design an offline-capable or paper-bridge workflow for locations where connectivity is intermittent
Escalate infrastructure gaps as program blockers, not IT complaints — frame them in terms of their impact on data quality and reporting
Why this happens
EMRs and reporting tools are frequently configured centrally without input from the teams who use them day-to-day
Workarounds — skipped fields, placeholder entries, parallel paper records — are often visible signs that the system doesn't fit the workflow
Recommended actions
Sit with a staff member and observe them using the system in real conditions — friction invisible in a meeting becomes obvious within minutes
Identify the fields with the highest skip or error rates and investigate whether they can be simplified, pre-populated, or removed
Treat workarounds as diagnostic signals: if everyone is bypassing the same step, that step is the problem, not the staff
Why this happens
Teams often operate across an EMR, a program-specific database, a government HMIS, and spreadsheets — with no integration between them
Each system may have been introduced by a different program or funder, with no coordination on data standards or interoperability
Recommended actions
Map all systems currently in use and identify where data is being entered more than once — quantify the duplication burden
Prioritize embedding X into a system the team already uses, rather than adding a new one
Escalate fragmentation to program and technology leadership — this is a structural problem individual teams cannot resolve on their own
Why this happens
Data quality tasks — record review, error correction, completeness checks — are assumed to happen alongside clinical work, but in practice are always displaced by it
Without a named owner, data quality belongs to everyone in principle and no one in practice
Recommended actions
Identify a named data focal point — a records officer, program assistant, or rotating role — and make the responsibility explicit
Be concrete about time: "We need 30–45 minutes per week for record review" is actionable; "please prioritize data quality" is not
If no protected time can be found, bring this to leadership as a resource question — the work cannot happen without time assigned to it
Note: if no protected time or named role exists, solving the tool or training problem will not be sufficient — the workflow itself needs to change.
How to use this guide — clinical quality improvement

When a clinical quality improvement initiative stalls, the reason most often given is that the team or department simply doesn't have time for it. It's worth pausing on that explanation — because "we don't have time" is rarely the full picture. What it usually means is that the team hasn't been able to prioritize this initiative over the other demands on their time. That's a meaningfully different problem, and one we can do something about.

This version is oriented toward clinical QI initiatives — adoption of clinical guidelines and protocols, changes to care pathways, audit and feedback cycles, mortality and morbidity review, infection prevention practices, and other efforts to improve the quality or safety of care delivered. Use it when a clinical team or department is struggling to adopt, implement, or sustain a quality improvement practice.

Set aside the "no time" explanation and work through the root causes below. For each one that resonates, dig into the sub-questions to identify what's actually in the way.

Starting question — reframe "we don't have time" as:
Why is the team or department not able to prioritize X over their other work?
Click a root cause below to explore deeper why questions and recommended actions.
Dig deeper: why does no accountability exist?
Why this happens
QI initiatives are often introduced as add-ons to clinical work, without being embedded in existing supervision or performance structures
Clinical teams are primarily measured on throughput — patient volumes, waiting times — rather than on adherence to protocols or participation in QI processes
Recommended actions
Work with clinical leadership to add a QI participation indicator to existing performance reviews — even tracking attendance at audit meetings creates visibility
Include X in clinical supervision agendas so it is regularly discussed as part of practice, not treated as a separate initiative
Start with observation and recognition rather than consequence — the goal is to normalize QI as part of clinical practice
Why this happens
QI initiatives promoted by program coordinators or quality officers don't carry the same weight with clinical staff as direction from a medical director, head nurse, or department chief
Senior clinicians may privately support X but haven't communicated this publicly or consistently to their teams
Recommended actions
Identify the clinical authority figure whose endorsement would matter most to this team and engage them directly — ask them to raise X explicitly in team meetings or ward rounds
Provide clinical leadership with brief talking points connecting X to patient outcomes they already care about
Why this happens
When clinical teams are under pressure to see more patients, QI activities — audits, case reviews, protocol training — are the first things displaced
The implicit message when only volume is measured is that quality processes are optional extras, not core clinical responsibilities
Recommended actions
Work with leadership to protect dedicated time for QI activities on the clinical schedule — even 30 minutes per week becomes sustainable if it's blocked
Make QI participation visible in the same reporting structures as volume metrics — track both, side by side
Where possible, connect X directly to efficiency gains that reduce volume pressure — QI and throughput don't have to be framed as opposites
Dig deeper: why is value not apparent?
Why this happens
Clinical guidelines developed at national or HQ level may not account for local epidemiology, resource constraints, or patient demographics — making them feel like boxes to tick rather than tools to use
Teams may have learned through experience that standard protocols need local adaptation — and feel frustrated when this isn't acknowledged
Recommended actions
Acknowledge the adaptation gap directly: invite the team to identify where the guideline fits their context and where it needs adjustment
Work toward a locally adapted version of the protocol that preserves the clinical intent while accounting for real constraints — and document the adaptations formally
Involve the clinical team in the QI design process from the start, not just the implementation phase
Why this happens
Evidence for clinical interventions often comes from high-resource settings — clinical staff may reasonably question whether it applies in their context
QI results from other sites may not have been shared with this team, so the evidence base feels abstract rather than demonstrated
Recommended actions
Find and share evidence or case examples from comparable settings — low-resource, high-burden, or geographically similar — rather than defaulting to high-income country studies
If internal data exists from other sites that have implemented X, share it specifically: "At a site with a similar patient load, this change led to..."
Offer to run a small pilot with this team to generate local evidence — a 90-day test with clear metrics is often more persuasive than any external study
Why this happens
Clinical teams that have participated in audit cycles or protocol rollouts that produced no visible change often approach new QI initiatives with guarded skepticism
If previous QI efforts were externally driven and disappeared when the project ended, the team has learned that these initiatives are temporary
Recommended actions
Name the pattern directly: "I know QI hasn't always led to change here — help me understand what got in the way before, so we can do this differently"
Design X to produce a visible clinical improvement within 60–90 days — something the team can point to as evidence the effort was worth it
Commit to a feedback loop: share what the QI process found and what will change as a result, every time
Why this happens
QI initiatives are often introduced through managers or department heads without a direct conversation with the nurses, clinicians, or support staff who will do the work
Written guidance — SOPs, policy memos, training manuals — may exist but isn't reaching or resonating with frontline staff
Recommended actions
Request time directly with the clinical team — not just the department head — to explain why X matters in plain, patient-centered language
Frame X around a clinical problem the team already recognizes: "This is about reducing the number of patients who come back with complications"
Identify a respected clinician on the team who is already convinced and invite them to champion X among peers — peer influence is more powerful than external advocacy
Dig deeper: what is blocking implementation?
Why this happens
Clinical guidelines describe what should happen, not how to make it happen in a specific setting — the implementation gap is real and often underestimated
Teams may understand the clinical standard but have no process for reviewing current practice, identifying gaps, and making targeted changes
Recommended actions
Offer a structured starting point: a brief baseline audit, a gap analysis tool, or a simple PDSA cycle template adapted to their context
Identify one specific clinical practice to change first — not "improve sepsis management" but "ensure all patients with fever have a triage time recorded within 15 minutes"
Support the first cycle closely, then step back — the goal is to build the team's own capacity to run QI, not dependency on external support
Why this happens
QI is often driven by coordinators, nurses, or program staff who do not have authority over physician or senior clinical practice — creating an awkward dynamic
Senior clinicians may resist process changes proposed by colleagues they perceive as junior, regardless of clinical merit
Recommended actions
Engage the medical director or department chief as the visible owner of X — the QI lead can drive the process, but the clinical authority figure needs to front it
Frame the QI process as peer review and shared learning rather than compliance — this is more culturally acceptable in clinical settings
Where possible, let the data speak: audit findings presented by peers carry more weight than directives from a coordinator
Why this happens
QI methodology — PDSA cycles, root cause analysis, audit and feedback — is not part of most clinical training curricula in the region
Asking a clinical team to "run a QI initiative" without equipping them with basic methods sets them up to stall at the first decision point
Recommended actions
Offer a brief, practical orientation to QI methods — 2–3 hours, focused on tools they will actually use, not a full curriculum
Provide a simple QI toolkit adapted to the clinical context: a one-page audit form, a basic data collection template, a problem statement guide
Pair the team with a mentor or peer from a site that has successfully run a QI cycle — learning from a clinical peer is often more effective than formal training
Why this happens
In high-burden clinical environments, every available hour goes to direct patient care — improvement work, which is inherently future-oriented, cannot compete with immediate need
QI activities that aren't scheduled as protected time are reliably displaced by clinical emergencies and volume surges
Recommended actions
Work with clinical leadership to block specific, recurring time for QI — even one hour per month that is genuinely protected from clinical duties
Design QI activities to be as lightweight as possible: a 10-item audit, a 20-minute case review, rather than a full-day workshop
If no time can be protected, escalate to leadership as a resource and staffing issue — QI cannot happen without it
Note: in high-burden clinical settings this is often the real constraint. Addressing it requires a leadership conversation about resourcing, not a motivation conversation with the clinical team.
Dig deeper: what is missing from the clinical environment?
Why this happens
National clinical guidelines are often written at a level of abstraction that requires significant interpretation to apply in a specific setting — a step that is rarely resourced
Different clinicians may be interpreting the same guideline differently, creating unintentional variation in practice that makes QI measurement difficult
Recommended actions
Work with the clinical team to develop a brief, locally adapted SOP — a one-page flowchart or decision aid that translates the guideline into the specific steps staff will follow at this location
Involve a mix of clinical roles in the adaptation process so the SOP reflects real workflow rather than an idealized one
Pilot the SOP with a small group, gather feedback, and revise before broader rollout — avoid treating the first draft as final
Why this happens
Clinical QI initiatives sometimes assume resource availability that doesn't exist — a hand hygiene protocol requiring a sink at every bedside, or a triage protocol requiring equipment that stockouts regularly
Teams may have tried to implement X before and found it impossible without the resources — their reluctance now reflects a rational response to that experience
Recommended actions
Before launching X, audit the specific resources it requires and confirm availability — don't assume the supply chain will support it without checking
Design a resource-adapted version of X for conditions of intermittent supply — define minimum requirements for full implementation versus a constrained alternative
Escalate supply and equipment gaps as clinical quality risks, not just procurement issues — this framing is more likely to prompt action
Why this happens
QI requires a recurring structure for data review, reflection, and decision-making — without this, it remains a one-off activity rather than an embedded practice
Facilities that have never had a functioning audit cycle or quality committee have no institutional home for QI work, making each initiative start from scratch
Recommended actions
Prioritize establishing a minimal recurring QI structure before launching X — a monthly 30-minute quality review meeting is a foundation everything else can build on
Attach X to an existing clinical meeting if one exists rather than creating a new one — reducing the scheduling burden
Support the facility to develop a simple terms of reference and agenda for a quality committee, so the structure outlasts any single initiative
Why this happens
In the absence of a named quality lead, QI responsibility is diffuse — nurses assume doctors own it, doctors assume the program team owns it, and nothing moves
Where a QI role exists on paper, it may not come with protected time, decision-making authority, or support from leadership
Recommended actions
Work with clinical leadership to name a specific person responsible for QI at this facility — ideally a respected clinician, not only an administrator
Define what the role entails concretely: which meetings to convene, which data to review, who to report to, and how often
Ensure the named person has explicit backing from the medical director or department head — a QI lead without authority cannot drive change in a clinical hierarchy
Note: naming a person without giving them time, authority, or support is unlikely to produce results — all three are needed together.
